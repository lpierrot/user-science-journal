Blogging About Design

Excited to share my thoughts and findings

Posted by Lionel Pierrot on April 20, 2019

Before the age of the technology finding a place to buy or rent was a challenging task. We mostly relied on newspapers, word of mouth, realtors, or just driving around town looking for "For Sale" or "For Rent" signs. In today's world though, well-designed apps and websites like Zillow have made this task effortless and less time-consuming.

Briefly, Zillow is a real estate and rental marketplace dedicated to helping consumers find home rentals, selling and buying homes. The site offers a lot more services as well, but for today I am only going to focus on my personal experience as a Zillow user and the overall design of the website. To accomplish my analysis, I will look at different criteria designers use to make usability as simple as possible and yet informative: navigation conventions, breadcrumbs, site identification, and page location.

Whose site is this?

Right off the bat, it is hard to miss the purpose or the name of the site. Every element on the home page reinforces what the website is all about. As a first time user looking at the homepage, right above and in the search bar itself, there’s enough information such the option to input the address, the city, and words like We’ll help you find a place you’ll love. for a new user to understand what the site offers.

Zillow's homepage

In the side of the sections of the website, you can also see options like Buy, Rent, Sell, Home loans, Agent finder. This information alone, I find it useful enough to help the user understand whose site they are on.

What page am I on?

Finding what page you are looking at on Zillow is a bit confusing. For example, when you click on rent and buy on the Section side of the site. It is hard to tell the difference between the two pages

Zillow buy button in the section area Zillow rent button in the section area

On the pictures above, it is hard to tell the difference between the two pages without having to go through settings and adjusting your searches. To avoid pushing away those users that are search-dominant this would be an excellent opportunity to add tabs on each page.
